The Policy Pillars That Support The Reform Model

APP means the Australian Prosperity Program - a reform model and policy development project.

APP is not a launched political party. This material is a discussion model, not a final election-costed platform.

The Reform Model page explains how the system works together. This page explains the policy pillars that support that model, using the current active Volume II policy chapters.

Each pillar still needs public testing, legal review, implementation design and final costing before it could become a platform.

APP Reform Program

The APP Reform Program is the front-door framework for Volume II. It says Australia should move from a revenue-and-distribution model to a revenue, distribution and wealth creation model.

The key change is the allocation order: wealth creation first, essential government functions second, and discretionary programs only where fiscal capacity allows.

The program adds a fourth long-term source of national income: investment returns from national wealth. It supports the wider reform model by making tax, spending, resource, housing, energy and infrastructure policy work toward the same aim: permanent national wealth, lower future tax pressure and stronger public finances.

Fiscal Framework

The Fiscal Framework sets the spending rules around the model. It separates essential government functions, wealth creation, the safety net, discretionary programs and permanent redistribution.

It supports a structural surplus rule in normal conditions, with deficits limited to recession, war, national emergency or severe natural disaster. Structural surpluses should automatically transfer to the Future Generations Fund, without election-year exceptions.

Debt should not fund recurring spending or political promises. The framework supports essential services and a humane safety net, but rejects permanent redistribution as the central purpose of government.

Tax Reform

Tax Reform resets the tax system around simpler and clearer rules. The current model includes a $40,000 tax-free threshold, a 25% personal income tax rate above the threshold, a 25% company tax rate, a broad 20% GST, no negative gearing, no CGT discount, and simplified deductions, offsets and concessions.

It keeps franking credits to avoid double taxation of company profits. It also includes stronger anti-avoidance rules for trusts, income splitting, related-party transactions, offshore profit shifting, transfer pricing and beneficial ownership transparency.

The problem it solves is a complex tax system that rewards navigation, loopholes and distortion. It supports the wider reform model by rewarding work and enterprise while helping fund essential services and the Future Generations Fund.

Future Generations Fund

The Future Generations Fund converts temporary resource wealth, structural surpluses and selected extraordinary receipts into permanent national assets. It is designed as an Australian adaptation of the Norwegian sovereign wealth principle, not a direct copy.

At least 50% of net resource rent revenues should flow to the Fund, and structural surpluses should transfer automatically after essential functions and prudential buffers are met. Capital withdrawals during the first 25 years should be prohibited except for extreme national emergencies under high-threshold rules.

After year 25, ordinary distributions may be made up to 3% of the rolling average Fund value, subject to capital preservation and independent oversight. The mature base-case contribution target is $75B; that is not a Year 1 amount and the model is not yet a fully ramped annual cashflow model.

The long-term objective in the chapter is a Fund worth between $3 trillion and $5 trillion over about 25 years. The Fund supports the reform model by reducing future dependence on taxation while protecting national wealth from short-term political consumption.

Resource Wealth Reform

Resource Wealth Reform is the machinery for capturing fair public value from finite resources. The active chapter presents a broad national wealth strategy, with the current numerical model still gas-focused.

The reform combines valuation integrity, a minimum public return, profit-based rent capture, project ring-fencing, public reporting and Future Generations Fund allocation. It includes PRRT repair, netback pricing where useful, baseline royalties, project-level reporting, transfer-pricing controls, beneficial ownership transparency and BEPS controls against related-party debt, offshore service charges, marketing fees, insurance, shipping and other profit-shifting channels.

At least 50% of net resource rent revenue should transfer to the Future Generations Fund. That allocation is part of the mature $75B base-case target, not an extra amount added on top, so it should not be double counted.

Gas revenue assumptions need validation. Non-gas resources are not yet numerically modelled, even though the policy framework could later apply to other nationally significant finite resources if Parliament chose to do so.

Housing

Housing policy aims to restore attainable ownership, improve affordability, support workforce mobility and guarantee housing security for Australians who genuinely need help. It treats housing security as a social foundation, while still keeping private ownership as a national objective.

The active Volume II mechanism is a Housing Security Scheme. Government retains ownership, eligible occupants receive secure long-term tenure, no rent is charged, there are no sale, mortgage or leverage rights, and the property returns to public ownership on death or relinquishment.

The chapter also supports expanding housing security stock, directly purchasing required social housing stock from developers where governments require allocations, faster planning approvals, infrastructure-linked growth, stamp duty replacement with a modest land-based framework, and restrictions on foreign ownership of established residential housing.

It supports the reform model by lowering one of the largest household cost pressures, improving mobility, supporting family formation and reducing pressure for temporary subsidies.

Family, Population And Migration

Family, Population and Migration policy links population growth to national capacity. Population growth is not treated as an end in itself; it should serve citizens, housing availability, infrastructure capacity, workforce requirements and the national interest.

The chapter supports family formation through affordable housing, lower taxation, lower living costs, accessible childcare, economic security and long-term confidence. Migration should be skills-based, selective, values-compatible, infrastructure-linked and focused on genuine shortages rather than wage suppression or replacing domestic workforce development.

It also supports a contribution-first welfare model for most non-essential welfare, with citizenship and roughly ten years of productive taxpaying participation as the general standard. This pillar supports the reform model by aligning migration, family policy, services and infrastructure instead of letting growth permanently outrun capacity.

Education

Education policy is about building capable citizens, skilled workers, independent thinkers and future leaders. Core education should give every child a fair baseline in literacy, numeracy, critical thinking, civic understanding and useful skills.

The chapter puts critical thinking first, followed by trades and vocational skills, academic excellence, personal development, civic education, employment readiness and national competitiveness. It gives trades equal respect with university pathways, supports apprenticeship support parity, and directs public support toward capability development rather than academic status.

Priority disciplines include medicine, engineering, science, technology, agriculture and teaching. Education supports the wider reform model by building the human capability needed for productivity, infrastructure, healthcare, energy, defence industries and long-term prosperity.

Energy

Energy policy aims to provide reliable, affordable and secure energy. The chapter starts from a clear problem: Australia should not be an energy-rich country with energy-poor households and businesses.

The policy hierarchy is reliability first, then energy independence, affordability, environmental stewardship and speed of deployment. It supports a diversified energy mix, staged nuclear power as long-term baseload generation, domestic gas reservation, strategic fuel reserves for diesel, petrol and aviation fuel, grid resilience, storage integration and industrial energy competitiveness.

Energy supports the reform model by lowering real household and business costs, strengthening national resilience and turning Australia's resource advantage into productive capacity rather than household pressure.

Infrastructure

Infrastructure policy treats infrastructure as an investment in national capability, not just spending. Projects should reduce costs, improve productivity, support growth, increase resilience and enable housing supply.

The chapter supports a national freight rail corridor linking regional Australia, capital cities, industrial centres, major ports and Western Sydney Airport. It also supports freight productivity, regional connectivity, housing-enabling infrastructure, Australian control of strategic assets, economic corridors and modern digital infrastructure.

Infrastructure supports the reform model by lowering hidden costs across food, housing, exports, transport and business activity. It also makes population growth, housing supply, energy security and regional development more workable.

Healthcare

Healthcare policy preserves universal access to essential healthcare while improving sustainability, efficiency and patient outcomes. Essential care should remain needs-blind, but not every payment, medicine, lifestyle service or low-value intervention is automatically essential.

The chapter supports Medicare sustainability, possible modest GP and specialist co-payments with exemptions for vulnerable Australians, public hospital reform, reduced Commonwealth-state duplication, private health insurance reform and stronger fraud controls.

It also links healthcare workforce planning to education and migration, especially for doctors, nurses, allied health professionals, specialists and regional healthcare. Healthcare supports the reform model by protecting a core service within clear boundaries so it remains affordable for future generations.

Welfare And NDIS

Welfare policy distinguishes emergency support, the essential safety net and long-term non-emergency welfare. It protects people in genuine hardship while maintaining public confidence that long-term benefits reflect membership, contribution and responsibility.

The chapter supports a citizen-first and contribution-first approach for most non-essential welfare, with emergency and essential services kept separate where life, safety, child protection, disaster response or severe disability require immediate support. It also supports workforce participation, training, apprenticeships, education, housing stability and pathways to independence where people can work.

NDIS and disability support should protect Australians with severe and permanent disabilities while resetting boundaries, reducing bureaucracy, preventing fraud, improving provider accountability, imposing price discipline and stopping waste, rorts and loosely defined supports.

NDIS assumptions need validation before they should be treated as firm savings numbers. This pillar supports the reform model by keeping the safety net humane, trusted and financially sustainable.

Government Reform

Government Reform aims to make government smaller, more effective, more accountable and more productive. The goal is not weaker government; it is government that is easier to understand, easier to use, cheaper to administer and harder to game.

The chapter supports a long-term reduction of about 20% in Australian Public Service staffing through attrition, automation, consolidation, process improvement and technology adoption. It also supports department consolidation, federal-state rationalisation, consultant reduction, grant review, regulatory simplification, digital government, outcome-based budgeting and 10-year sunset reviews for major programs, agencies and regulations.

Government efficiency assumptions need validation. This pillar supports the wider reform model by reducing duplication, lowering compliance costs, improving delivery and freeing resources for lower taxes, stronger services and the Future Generations Fund.

Justice

Justice policy protects law-abiding citizens, upholds the rule of law and maintains public confidence. It rejects two-tier justice, identity-based treatment, political favour and unequal institutional protection.

The policy hierarchy is deterrence, community protection, restitution, punishment and rehabilitation. The chapter supports stronger victim recognition, meaningful sentencing, repeat offender controls, fraud enforcement, anti-corruption rules, court efficiency reform, police resources and correctional accountability.

Justice supports the reform model by protecting equal citizenship, property rights, public trust, investment confidence and community safety.

Defence

Defence policy aims to preserve sovereignty, deter aggression, protect national interests and build resilience. The central idea is deterrence through capability, preparedness and the ability to withstand international instability.

The chapter supports maintaining current defence expenditure levels in the initial reform phase, with future increases considered as finances strengthen and circumstances evolve. It also supports fuel, food, energy, medicine, essential supply and critical input reserves; sovereign defence industry where practical; maritime security; northern Australia preparedness; voluntary national service; civil defence; cyber capability; and value-for-money procurement.

Defence supports the reform model by protecting the security and resilience that prosperity depends on, while keeping capability growth tied to fiscal strength.

Foreign Affairs

Foreign Affairs and Trade policy advances sovereignty, prosperity, security and stable international relationships. It supports an Australia-first approach guided by national interest, independent decision-making and practical engagement.

The chapter supports open trade where practical, export growth, investment opportunities, trade diversification, economic diplomacy, regional engagement and alliance management. On China, it supports constructive economic engagement, respectful diplomacy, strategic caution and diversification of key dependencies.

It also supports Australian control of strategic assets, supply-chain security, sovereign capability in critical industries and accountable development assistance. This pillar supports the reform model by protecting the external conditions needed for trade, investment, energy security, strategic resilience and long-term national wealth.

How The Pillars Fit Together

These pillars are not separate promises floating on their own.

Tax reform simplifies incentives. Fiscal discipline protects the budget. Resource reform captures fair value. The Future Generations Fund preserves it. Housing, energy, infrastructure, education and healthcare lower structural pressure and build capability. Welfare, government reform, justice, defence and foreign affairs protect trust, resilience and sustainability.

The full economic model belongs on the Reform Model and 25-Year Plan pages. Policy Foundations is the public map of the policy machinery that supports that model.
