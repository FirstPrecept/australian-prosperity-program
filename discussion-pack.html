Discussion Pack

A briefing page for parties, independents, policy committees, advisers and civic organisations.

Purpose

The Australian Prosperity Program is a reform model for discussion and testing.

This discussion pack is for political parties, independents, policy committees, advisers and civic organisations that want to assess the Australian Prosperity Program (APP) as a serious reform model.

It is not a final election-costed platform. It is a structured proposal for review.

One-Page Summary

The Australian Prosperity Program shifts Australia from a tax-and-distribution model to a national wealth creation model.

  • tax reset;
  • spending discipline;
  • Resource Wealth Reform;
  • Future Generations Fund;
  • structural cost-of-living reform;
  • Welfare/NDIS sustainability and boundary reform;
  • Government efficiency, duplication reduction, workforce reform and administrative simplification;
  • long-term implementation discipline.

The tax reset is based on equal rates, simple rules, fewer workarounds and removal of hidden cost taxes.

The Policy Problem

Australia faces rising pressure from complex taxes, rising spending, housing stress, energy insecurity, healthcare and disability cost growth, weak resource wealth conversion, short-term budget politics and future tax pressure.

The current model asks future taxpayers to carry more pressure without building enough permanent national wealth.

The Proposed Shift

  1. simplify and reset the tax system;
  2. control spending growth;
  3. capture fair value from finite resources;
  4. invest a defined share into a Future Generations Fund;
  5. protect essential services;
  6. reduce future tax pressure through long-term national assets.

The model treats inflationary tax removal as a structural cost-of-living reform, not just a tax cut, because embedded taxes can pass through and compound across prices, transactions, business costs and supply chains.

Key Numbers

Base case $75B mature annual Future Generations Fund contribution target; approximately $4.7437T Future Generations Fund value at Year 25; approximately $142B annual 3% drawdown capacity after Year 25.

$4.7437T is the current base-case Year 25 model output within the broader $3T-$5T long-term target range.

Important: $75B is not a Year 1 assumption. Early contributions may be modest. The model is staged and timing-aware, but not yet a fully ramped annual cashflow model.

Current Caveats

  • NDIS savings require confirmation.
  • Government efficiency savings require confirmation.
  • Gas revenue assumptions require further validation.
  • Non-gas resource wealth is not yet numerically modelled.
  • Qualitative cost-of-living, housing, infrastructure and energy effects are not yet numerically modelled.
  • Conservative and optimistic cases are sensitivity cases only.

Questions For Reviewers

Economic Model

Is the $75B mature contribution target plausible? What ramp profile should be tested? How should gas revenue be validated?

Tax Reform

What transition issues arise from the threshold, rate structure, broad GST and deduction simplification? Which inflationary taxes should be removed first? How should stamp duty and payroll tax transition be negotiated with states? What timing is needed for excise removal? How should the model estimate pass-through and compounding effects from payroll tax, stamp duty, fuel excise and other embedded taxes? Does a flat 25% rate above the tax-free threshold improve work, overtime, promotion and enterprise incentives? Which deductions, offsets, rebates or concessions should remain because they have a clear public purpose? How should the model test distributional effects while keeping the principle of equal rates? How should the model assess high marginal tax rates, gross wage demands, business costs and price pressure?

Implementation

Is the Year 1 legislative program too large? Which reforms must happen first? What should be delayed?

Suggested Discussion Format

  1. Start with the reform model, not the headline number.
  2. Test the implementation sequence.
  3. Test the provisional assumptions.
  4. Review the political and public acceptance risks.
  5. Decide which parts deserve independent modelling.

Suggested Outcome

The best next step is not immediate adoption.

The best next step is serious testing: independent fiscal review, staged implementation review, gas revenue validation, NDIS and government efficiency savings review, and public-facing explanation testing.
