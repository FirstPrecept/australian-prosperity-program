Key Assumptions

The current numbers, caveats and open assumptions behind the discussion model.

Status of the model This framework is a discussion model for serious review. It is not a final election-costed platform. It is designed to test whether the Australian Prosperity Program deserves further review by political parties, independents, policy committees and advisers.

Resource Wealth Assumptions

The current numerical Resource Wealth Reform model is gas-focused only.

  • gas royalty floor / rent capture;
  • PRRT repair / netback pricing;
  • valuation integrity;
  • base erosion and profit shifting controls;
  • transfer-pricing controls;
  • 50% net gas resource-rent allocation to the Future Generations Fund.

The 50% allocation is part of the $75B mature contribution target. It is not additional to the $75B target.

PRRT means Petroleum Resource Rent Tax. Netback pricing estimates resource value by working back from final sale prices after transport and processing costs. Base erosion and profit shifting means moving profits to reduce tax.

Non-Gas Resources

Non-gas resources are not yet numerically modelled.

  • iron ore;
  • coal;
  • critical minerals;
  • other nationally significant finite resources.

The doctrine supports broader Resource Wealth Reform. The current numerical model does not yet include those revenue streams.

Tax Reform Assumptions

The current tax reset assumes:

  • a $40,000 tax-free threshold for personal labour income actually earned by the worker;
  • the threshold does not apply to passive income, investment income, company distributions, trust distributions, income-splitting structures or income allocated to children;
  • a 25% personal income tax rate above the threshold;
  • a 25% company tax rate;
  • a broad 20% GST;
  • immediate removal of Commonwealth inflationary taxes, including fuel excise, alcohol excise, tobacco excise and luxury car tax;
  • federal transition reimbursements to enable states to remove payroll tax and stamp duty;
  • negative gearing abolished as a personal deduction against labour income, with no grandfathering;
  • no CGT discount;
  • no personal deductions, rebates or offsets against earned labour income;
  • the $40,000 threshold and 25% flat rate replace the personal deduction system for earned labour income;
  • genuine business input costs remain a separate business-tax design issue and are not treated as personal deductions against earned labour income;
  • passive income outside compliant superannuation is taxed at the ordinary APP rate of 25%;
  • child passive income and child trust distributions are taxed at the ordinary APP rate of 25%, without punitive minor rates and without access to the labour-income threshold;
  • investment earnings inside compliant superannuation remain tax-free in retirement phase, as they are today, with no additional tax on withdrawals after preservation or retirement age;
  • superannuation contribution, salary-sacrifice, related-party transfer, early-access and large-balance rules require further design to prevent superannuation becoming a general tax shelter;
  • testamentary trusts require separate legal design where they serve genuine estate, child, disability, dependency or vulnerable-beneficiary protection purposes;
  • any Estate Transfer Credit or equivalent mechanism requires further modelling, valuation rules, eligibility rules, treatment of income and capital, anti-avoidance rules and legal drafting.

The model assumes high marginal tax rates can reduce the after-tax reward from additional work and may add upward pressure to gross wage demands.

Further modelling should test behavioural effects, distributional impacts, wage-pressure effects and transition issues.

Tax Reform is assumed to be legislated in Year 1, transitioned through Years 1-3, and fully operating by Year 3.

Personal Income Tax Reset Assumption

The model treats the personal income tax reform as a single net reset rather than a stack of separate tax-expenditure add-backs.

The current base case uses:

  • Personal Income Tax Reset: -$56B.

This is a conservative estimate of the net impact of moving current personal income-tax receipts to the APP labour-income tax model.

The estimate assumes:

  • a 25% personal income tax rate;
  • a $40,000 tax-free threshold limited to personal labour income actually earned by the worker;
  • no threshold access for passive income, investment income, company distributions, trust distributions, child allocations or income-splitting structures;
  • no personal deductions, rebates or offsets against earned labour income;
  • negative gearing abolished as a personal deduction against labour income;
  • the CGT discount removed as a simplification measure, not as a major revenue source.

Because these effects are captured in the personal income tax reset, the model does not separately count major revenue gains from CGT discount removal, negative gearing abolition, personal deduction removal, trust threshold blocking or income-splitting controls.

This avoids double-counting current-law tax expenditure estimates after the personal tax system has already been replaced by a 25% labour-income tax model.

Self-Funded Retiree Assumption

The $40,000 tax-free threshold applies to personal labour income actually earned by the worker. It does not apply to passive income, investment income, trust distributions, company distributions or income allocated to children.

The model protects retirement primarily through compliant superannuation settings. Investment earnings inside compliant retirement-phase superannuation remain tax-free, and withdrawals after preservation or retirement age are not additionally taxed.

Passive income outside superannuation is taxed at the ordinary APP rate of 25% and does not receive the labour-income threshold.

This creates a pressure-test issue for low-income self-funded retirees whose savings sit outside superannuation for legacy reasons. The model should consider whether a narrow transition rule is needed for this group, but it should not create a general passive-income threshold or a new tax shelter for asset-rich retirees, trusts or income-splitting structures.

Inflationary Tax Removal Assumptions

  • The model includes a pathway to remove or phase down taxes that raise structural costs, including stamp duty, payroll tax, fuel excise, luxury car tax and selected excises.
  • The model assumes that some taxes have pass-through and compounding effects across prices, transactions, business costs and supply chains.
  • Commonwealth-controlled taxes can be addressed through federal legislation.
  • State taxes such as stamp duty and payroll tax require federal-state transition design.
  • The exact timing, compensation to states if any, and revenue replacement pathway require further modelling.
  • Further modelling should estimate both direct revenue impacts and broader cost-base reductions for households and businesses.
  • These reforms should be treated as cost-of-living and economic-efficiency measures, not as isolated tax cuts.

Welfare, NDIS And Government Efficiency

The model uses the phrase NDIS sustainability and boundary reform. The aim is to protect genuine disability support while reducing excessive growth, provider leakage, weak scheme boundaries and loosely defined supports.

Government efficiency means duplication reduction, workforce reform and administrative simplification. It is not framed as an arbitrary cut target.

Validation required NDIS savings, government efficiency savings and gas revenue assumptions require confirmation.

Qualitative Effects Not Yet Numerically Modelled

  • structural cost-of-living relief;
  • infrastructure productivity uplift;
  • energy affordability and sovereignty benefits;
  • housing affordability effects;
  • reduced compliance costs from simplicity and reset.
