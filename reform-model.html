How The Reform Model Works As One System

The Australian Prosperity Program (APP) is a discussion model for national wealth creation.

This page explains how the system works together: how tax simplicity, spending discipline, fair resource returns, a protected Future Generations Fund, structural cost-of-living relief and staged implementation interact as one model.

The model asks a simple question: how can Australia move from repeated tax pressure and short-term budget management toward a system that builds permanent national wealth?

The answer is not one policy by itself. It is the interaction between tax simplicity, spending discipline, fair resource returns, a protected Future Generations Fund, structural cost-of-living relief and staged implementation.

The System Logic

The reform model works in sequence.

  1. Tax settings are simplified so work, enterprise, saving and investment face clearer incentives.
  2. Spending discipline prevents reform gains from being absorbed by permanent expansion of programs without limits.
  3. Resource Wealth Reform captures fair public value from finite resources, beginning with the current gas-focused numerical model.
  4. A defined share of resource value and reform gains is preserved through the Future Generations Fund rather than consumed immediately.
  5. Long-term national wealth helps reduce future tax pressure, protect essential services and create structural cost-of-living relief.

Tax Simplicity

The tax reset is designed to make the system easier to understand, harder to game and more supportive of productive effort.

The current public model includes a $40,000 tax-free threshold, a 25% personal income tax rate above the threshold, a 25% company tax rate, a broad 20% GST, removal of negative gearing, removal of the CGT discount, and simplified deductions, offsets and concessions.

The principle is simple: one clear rate, fewer exceptions and fewer workarounds. People who earn more naturally pay more in total dollars, but the model avoids taking a higher proportion of each additional dollar as income rises.

This matters because high marginal tax rates reduce the after-tax reward from extra work, overtime, promotion, enterprise and risk. When the state takes a higher share of each extra dollar, extra work needs a higher gross wage to deliver the same after-tax reward. That can add upward pressure to wage demands, business costs and prices. A flatter system lets people keep a consistent share of each additional dollar.

The model also rejects the deduction maze as normal tax design. Deductions, offsets, rebates and concessions should only remain where there is a clear public purpose. The aim is a system that is simple enough for ordinary Australians to understand and hard for special interests to game.

Tax reform is not treated as a stand-alone promise. It only works inside a wider model that controls spending, broadens the base and builds national assets over time.

Inflationary Tax Removal

Cost-of-living relief is not treated as another temporary payment cycle. The model removes or phases down taxes that raise prices, increase transaction costs or hide revenue collection inside everyday activity.

This includes stamp duty, payroll tax, fuel excise, luxury car tax and selected excises, subject to fiscal capacity and federal-state transition design.

The reason this matters is pass-through. These taxes are built into the price of housing, employment, transport, goods and services. A tax on one input becomes part of the next price, and that higher price can then be marked up, taxed or passed on again. Fuel excise is especially important because transport is part of almost every supply chain.

Stamp duty raises the cost of buying and moving home. Payroll tax raises the cost of employing people and is recovered through prices, lower wage capacity, reduced hiring or lower investment. Excises raise the direct cost of taxed goods, and fuel excise flows through freight, groceries, construction, trades, logistics and services.

Spending Discipline

Spending discipline is the guardrail that makes the model credible.

The model supports a humane safety net and strong essential services, but it rejects permanent redistribution as the central purpose of government.

The model prioritises essential functions, genuine hardship support, national capability, infrastructure and long-term wealth creation.

Without spending discipline, tax reform becomes unstable and the Future Generations Fund becomes vulnerable to short-term political consumption.

Resource Wealth Reform

Resource Wealth Reform is the mechanism for capturing fair public value from finite resources.

The current numerical model is gas-focused and includes a gas royalty floor / rent capture, PRRT repair, netback pricing, valuation integrity, BEPS controls, transfer-pricing controls, project-level reporting, and allocation of 50% of net gas resource-rent revenue to the Future Generations Fund.

PRRT means Petroleum Resource Rent Tax. Netback pricing estimates resource value by working back from final sale prices after transport and processing costs. BEPS means base erosion and profit shifting, where profits are moved to reduce tax.

Contribution treatment The 50% allocation is part of the $75B mature base-case Future Generations Fund contribution target. It is not added on top of that target and it is not a Year 1 figure. Broader non-gas resources are not yet numerically modelled.

Future Generations Fund

The Future Generations Fund is the institution that turns temporary or finite reform gains into permanent national assets.

It is not a short-term spending account.

Its role is to preserve and invest a defined share of national wealth so future Australians inherit lower tax pressure, stronger public finances and greater resilience.

The $75B figure should be read as a mature base-case annual contribution target once the model is fully implemented and validated, not as an immediate annual cashflow.

Structural Cost-Of-Living Relief

The model does not rely on temporary cost-of-living handouts alone.

It aims to reduce structural pressure through simpler tax, inflationary tax removal, better housing supply, more affordable and reliable energy, infrastructure that lowers hidden costs, lower compliance burdens, and future tax-pressure reduction from national wealth.

Some benefits remain qualitative until assumptions are approved and independently tested.

Implementation Timing

The reform model is staged because the largest gains do not arrive immediately.

Tax reform, spending discipline, resource reform and the Future Generations Fund need sequencing, transition rules, institutional safeguards and annual cashflow modelling.

The model is not yet a fully ramped annual cashflow model.

NDIS, government efficiency and gas revenue assumptions need validation before they should be treated as firm budget numbers.

Delayed Payoff

The model accepts that long-term reform has a delayed payoff.

Early years would focus on legislation, institutional design, transition management, budget discipline and validation of assumptions.

The national wealth effect grows as resource returns are captured, fund contributions accumulate and investment earnings compound.

That delay is the point: the model shifts Australia from consuming current revenue to building an asset base for future taxpayers.

Scenarios

Conservative

Assumes slower implementation, lower resource revenue, tighter savings and more cautious fund contributions.

Base

Uses the mature $75B Future Generations Fund contribution target once reforms are fully ramped and validated.

Optimistic

Tests stronger resource returns, larger efficiency gains and faster growth, but should not be treated as the default case.

National Wealth Creation

The purpose of the whole model is national wealth creation.

Tax simplicity improves incentives. Spending discipline protects the budget. Resource Wealth Reform captures fair value from finite assets. The Future Generations Fund preserves that value. Structural reform lowers pressure on households and businesses over time.

Together, these parts create a model aimed at stronger services, lower future tax pressure, more resilient public finances and a better inheritance for future Australians.

A Model For Testing

This is a discussion model.

It needs scrutiny, independent testing, better annual ramp modelling and validation of major assumptions.

But it offers a serious alternative to a system that keeps asking current taxpayers to carry more pressure without building enough permanent national wealth.
