The 25-Year Plan

A long-term pathway from implementation and transition to national wealth accumulation and prudent drawdown.

Why Twenty-Five Years?

The Australian Prosperity Program (APP) is a long-term national wealth model.

It cannot be judged only by a first-year budget result.

Tax reform, spending discipline, resource revenue capture, housing reform, energy security and national wealth accumulation all take time.

The 25-year plan recognises that serious reform has a transition period.

A Clear End Point

Many Australians are disengaged from politics and governance because they do not see a serious long-term plan.

The 25-year plan gives the reform model an end point: lower future tax pressure, stronger services, national wealth and a better future.

It is a pathway out of the current tax-and-cost spiral and away from permanent short-term crisis management.

The plan is based on a first-principles view of government. Government should do the things citizens cannot do alone: protect the nation, uphold law and order, build infrastructure, provide shared services, steward national wealth, protect equal citizenship, provide essential services and a genuine safety net, and build long-term national capability.

Government should be a trusted steward serving all Australians - protecting citizens, building shared prosperity, and never treating people as subjects to manage or resources to mine.

Mature Base Case Budget Impact

The 25-year plan is not based on the full $75B Future Generations Fund contribution appearing in Year 1.

At full maturity, the current base case estimates the annual budget impact as follows:

This is a mature-state estimate, not a Year 1 result.

What Drives The Calculation

Additional costs and revenue losses include:

  • Personal Income Tax Reset: -$56B;
  • the 25% company tax rate;
  • removal of Commonwealth inflationary taxes, including fuel and other excises;
  • Housing Security Scheme costs;
  • freight rail phase costs;
  • nuclear establishment phase costs;
  • strategic reserves;
  • healthcare transition support;
  • state tax transition support.

Spending savings include:

  • NDIS sustainability and boundary reform;
  • government efficiency and administrative footprint reduction;
  • public service wage and benefit alignment;
  • grants and market-intervention reductions;
  • welfare eligibility reform;
  • duplication removal;
  • consolidation of overlapping service streams into needs-based services;
  • consultant, procurement and process savings;
  • ATO simplification dividend.

Revenue increases include:

  • broad 20% GST;
  • stronger transfer-pricing, beneficial-ownership and offshore profit-shifting controls;
  • gas-focused Resource Wealth Reform.

CGT discount removal, negative gearing abolition, personal deduction removal and trust/income-allocation controls are treated as part of the structural personal tax reset, not as separate major revenue add-backs.

The current model still requires validation of NDIS savings, government efficiency savings, gas revenue assumptions and the year-by-year ramp from Year 1 to maturity.

Year 1

Year 1 is an establishment and reset year.

The priority is to legislate and establish the machinery:

  • Fiscal Framework;
  • full Tax Reform legislation;
  • Resource Wealth Reform;
  • Future Generations Fund;
  • Government efficiency audit;
  • Welfare/NDIS reset kickoff;
  • cost-of-living reform program.

The Future Generations Fund begins in Year 1. Early contributions may be modest.

Years 1-3

This is the tax transition period. The model moves toward the $40,000 tax-free threshold for personal labour income actually earned by the worker, 25% personal income tax above the threshold, 25% company tax, broad 20% GST, negative gearing abolished as a personal deduction against labour income, no CGT discount, elimination of personal deductions, rebates and offsets against earned labour income, and the $40,000 threshold and 25% rate replacing the personal deduction system for earned labour income.

Begin the inflationary tax removal pathway, starting with Commonwealth-controlled taxes and transition design for state taxes.

Savings and revenue gains are partial and rising.

Years 3-25

From Years 3-5, reform maturity begins and federal-state transition arrangements progress for stamp duty and payroll tax reduction or replacement. From Years 5-10, the mature fiscal operating model starts to take shape. From Years 10-20, the Fund compounds and national strategic capacity becomes more important. From Years 20-25, the model prepares for drawdown readiness.

Year 25+

After Year 25, ordinary drawdowns may occur only under strict rules.

The preferred benchmark is a prudent 3% drawdown rule, subject to capital protection, independent oversight, parliamentary reporting, conservative modelling and high-threshold withdrawal controls.

Why The Delay Matters

The model is honest about timing. Not all savings arrive in Year 1. Not all revenue gains arrive in Year 1. The Future Generations Fund does not instantly receive the mature $75B annual contribution.

The long-term result depends on disciplined implementation over time.

Conservative, Base And Optimistic Scenarios

Conservative

Lower reform delivery, lower savings, slower contribution growth and more cautious fund accumulation.

Base

The current canonical model. It uses the $75B mature contribution target, 7% return and approximately $4.7437T Year 25 value. $4.7437T is the current base-case Year 25 model output within the broader $3T-$5T long-term target range.

Optimistic

Stronger reform delivery, higher savings, stronger gas resource-rent capture and stronger contribution performance.

Main Caveat

Annual cashflow caveat The current model is staged and timing-aware, but it is not yet a fully ramped annual cashflow model. A future model should show year-by-year ramp assumptions for tax transition, savings delivery, gas revenue capture and Future Generations Fund contributions.
